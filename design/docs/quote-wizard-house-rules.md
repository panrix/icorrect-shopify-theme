# Quote Wizard — House Rules

The conventions every flow follows. One page, so anyone building the rest stays consistent.

**The point of all of it:** more trust, higher conversion. We win the right customers by telling the truth — not by hiding the price, and not by turning business away. Qualifying out a bad-fit job is in service of conversion, never against it.

---

## The aim, stated plainly

Speak to every customer; gently put off the ones we genuinely can't help. But the goal is a **higher** conversion rate, not a lower one. Transparency *is* the conversion engine — people book because we were straight with them. The deflection saves the wasted trip, the bad review, and the dead-end intake, and the people who come through are pre-sold on both the price and the honesty.

---

## The rules

1. **Two paths, one spine.** Path A — the decided customer wants a price fast, so don't make them talk. Path B — the unsure customer needs triage and reassurance first. Device, model and booking are shared underneath.

2. **Band-movers only.** Ask a question only if the answer changes the outcome or the number. If every answer lands in the same place, cut it.

3. **Ask what they can see; disclose what they can't.** Never gate a price on something the customer can't know — like hidden screen damage. Set the floor from what's visible, disclose the ceiling to everyone.

4. **Every path lands on a number or a next action.** No dead-end "bring it in." Firm price, honest band, free fix, or a clear redirect — always something.

5. **The estimate spectrum.** Firm quote when the symptom names the part. Honest band when the repair is only probable. Pure diagnostic only when it's genuinely unknowable — and keep that rare.

6. **Anchor high.** Quote toward the top of the range. The bench result should be a relief, never a shock. Under-promise the price, over-deliver at the bench.

7. **Show your working.** An estimate is a structure, not a number: core repair + likely add-ons + the disclosed worst case. Itemised honesty is what earns the booking.

8. **Agency removes the rip-off feeling.** The expensive part is always the customer's choice, never our verdict — "replace it, or we fix the rest and you live with it for now."

9. **Diagnostic fee credited to the repair.** Always say it. It removes the "paying twice" fear.

10. **Five outcomes, one vocabulary.** QUOTE ✓ · ESTIMATE · FIXED AT HOME · DATA RECOVERY · REDIRECT. Every flow uses the same tags.

11. **Wizard is not the bench.** Customer-answerable questions live in the wizard. Tool-or-teardown questions (ammeter, DCPS, IMEI) live in the diagnostic system. One source, two homes.

12. **Keep it current.** Symptom order tracks this quarter's repair mix, not last year's. Review periodically — flexgate was once top of the list and is now 1%.

13. **Cross-link, don't duplicate.** Liquid is a hard redirect, not a copied branch. Shared booking spine, no parallel wizards.

14. **Safety first where it matters.** Liquid: power off, don't charge, come in fast. Lead with care before cost.

## How the routes fit together

Two sides — but they're not parallel. One feeds the other.

**The quote side** answers *"how much?"* The fault is already known; we're resolving the price. Two gears:
- **Firm quote** — normal repairs. The symptom names the part. Exact price.
- **Estimate quote** — liquid, power, board-level. Can't commit to an exact figure, so: an honest band now, the £59 diagnostic to confirm it, credited to the repair.

**The diagnose side** answers *"what is it, and can you fix it?"* The fault is unknown; we're deciphering symptoms to name the likely fault and whether it's repairable at all. It produces a verdict, not a number.

The diagnose side sits **upstream** of the quote side — it's the on-ramp for people who can't classify themselves, and it ends by routing them into a firm quote, an estimate quote, a fix-at-home, or a not-us. Known fault → price it. Unknown fault → name it first, then price it.

## Qualify on the web, close at the bench

The website estimate is **not** where we close — it's where we qualify. The bench is where we close. Keeping those two jobs separate is what lets the team stop hesitating over the number.

- **The web qualifies.** An honest range — not silence, and not a cold flat figure. The floor filters out the people who'd never be happy at the real price: a good loss, taken at the cheapest moment instead of after a wasted trip. The ceiling is disclosed, not shouted, so a high bench result is never an ambush.
- **The diagnostic closes.** The £59 buys the four things a web page can't: time, evidence (the device open in front of them), a relationship, and the guarantee. A big number lands there when it would never land cold.
- **The range is what keeps it honest.** They came in already knowing it could reach the top of the band, so the in-person reveal is a confirmation, not a bait-and-switch. Diagnostic credited to the repair — always said.

**Putting people off — the right amount.** The honest floor is *allowed* to turn away the never-going-to-convert. Protect the borderline-but-winnable: don't set the floor so high or the ceiling so loud that the website scares off someone the bench could have won.

---

## The one-line test for any new question

> Does the customer's answer change what we tell them — and can they actually answer it from where they're sitting?

If no to either, it doesn't belong in the wizard.
